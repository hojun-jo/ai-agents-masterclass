The long-running border dispute between Cambodia and Thailand broke into open conflict in May and December 2025, causing over 100 deaths and displacing hundreds of thousands of people. A fragile ceasefire and continued sporadic skirmishes followed, while mistrust still reigns. This conflict exposes the challenges faced by ASEAN in maintaining regional peace and credibility. The conflict traces back to colonial-era boundary issues from poorly drawn 1907 French maps, centering on a sacred mountaintop site with the Preah Vihear temple, considered a symbol of national identity for both countries.

The Preah Vihear temple itself was ruled Cambodian territory by the International Court of Justice (ICJ) in 1962, but adjacent areas remain disputed. Tensions flared numerous times, with major flare-ups in 2008–2011 and again in 2025, involving artillery, air strikes, and ground skirmishes, resulting in casualties and displacement.

In July 2025, a full-scale armed conflict resulted in at least 32 deaths. Diplomatic efforts mediated by ASEAN and external actors like the US resulted in temporary ceasefires, but these have been fragile and frequently broken. The conflict illustrates ASEAN’s struggles with its non-interventionist principle and the limitations of regional security mechanisms.

Economic sanctions such as US tariffs were also imposed temporarily related to the conflict. The dynamics of great-power competition—particularly between the US and China—amplify the regional security complexities, while both Cambodia and Thailand remain locked in nationalist posturing. Resolution of the conflict remains uncertain due to deep-rooted historical grievances and political circumstances.

## Article 2: Thailand and Cambodia are bickering over their borders again. Can diplomacy prevent a return to war?  
**Source:** The Conversation  
**Date:** May 2026  
**URL:** https://theconversation.com/thailand-and-cambodia-are-bickering-over-their-borders-again-can-diplomacy-prevent-a-return-to-war-282589  
**Category:** International, Politics  
**Credibility Score:** 9  
**Relevance Score:** 8  

Last year, Thailand and Cambodia fought two brief conflicts over their 800-km-long land border that killed dozens and displaced half a million people. The dispute has escalated again, now focusing on their maritime border in the Gulf of Thailand. The cancellation of a 2001 maritime Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) by Thailand has heightened tensions, prompting Cambodia to initiate dispute resolution proceedings under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS).

The conflicting claims mainly arise from differing interpretations of colonial treaties and adjacent island sovereignty, with significant untapped oil and gas reserves in the Pattani Basin at stake. The 2001 MOU functioned as an agreement to negotiate joint development but was terminated due to stalled progress and repeated border clashes.

Cambodia’s recent ratification of UNCLOS allows it to use compulsory conciliation, a binding but non-decisive legal process that had previously helped resolve the Timor Sea dispute between Australia and Timor-Leste. However, Thailand's acceptance of this process is uncertain, given its rejection of ICJ jurisdiction for land disputes and political concerns about maritime claims.

The article stresses the high stakes of conflict escalation but highlights the opportunity that international legal frameworks and diplomatic engagement present for a peaceful resolution. It suggests that Cambodia’s choice for dispute resolution via diplomacy rather than war is a hopeful sign amid rising nationalism and past armed conflict.

Hostilities along the forested Thai-Cambodian border have paused intermittently, but a fierce information war rages between the two nations, fueled by social media, propaganda, and historical grievances. The border conflict escalated dramatically in July 2025 when Cambodia fired rockets into Thailand, prompting Thai air strikes. Both sides have suffered casualties and civilian displacement amid ongoing nationalist fervor.

The article details the complicated political dynamics within Thailand and Cambodia, including internal divisions in Thailand's government and Cambodia’s strong centralized control under Hun Sen and his son Hun Manet. The use of disinformation campaigns by Cambodia via state media and social media contrast with Thailand's official, but fragmented, government responses.

The centuries-old dispute centers on the Preah Vihear temple and adjacent territory, with multiple attempts at bilateral negotiation thwarted by nationalist sentiments and allegations of landmine use and shelling on both sides. Cambodia has escalated the issue to the United Nations Security Council and the International Court of Justice, which Thailand refuses to recognize in this dispute.

The article underscores how the conflict has severely strained relations, displaced hundreds of thousands of people, and contributed to economic and political instability, raising questions about the viability of resolving the dispute peacefully without sustained international mediation.
