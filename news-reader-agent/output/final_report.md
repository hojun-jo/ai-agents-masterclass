## 🚨 Today's Lead Story  
### Cambodia launches UNCLOS conciliation after Thailand scraps maritime MOU  
Cambodia has formally initiated compulsory conciliation under UNCLOS’s dispute-resolution procedures after Thailand withdrew from the 2001 maritime Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) that had provided for joint development of contested waters in the Gulf of Thailand. The May 2026 move hinges on Article 298 of UNCLOS, obliging Thailand to engage in a time-bound conciliation process—even though the final recommendations are non-binding. Phnom Penh seeks a peaceful alternative to armed conflict and aims to secure a clear legal framework for exploiting Pattani Basin oil and gas reserves. Bangkok, however, has signaled reticence: it recognizes neither the International Court of Justice for land disputes nor compulsory conciliation for maritime claims, fearing domestic backlash and potential loss of sovereign territory. Analysts note parallels with the 2016–17 Timor Sea case, where Australia and Timor-Leste’s conciliation led to a non-binding yet practical boundary framework and revenue-sharing deal. As both sides prepare delegations and conciliators, the process will test whether legal mechanisms can outflank rising nationalism and avert a return to kinetic hostilities.
