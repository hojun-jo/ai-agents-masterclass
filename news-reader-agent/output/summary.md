## Article 1: Cambodian-Thai conflict exposes ASEAN limits  
**Source:** GIS Reports  
**Date:** December 24, 2025  
**Original URL:** https://www.gisreportsonline.com/r/cambodian-thai-conflict-asean-limits/  

### 📱 Headline Summary (Tweet-length)  
Recurring Khmer-Thai border clashes in May and December 2025 have killed over 100 people, displaced half a million, and exposed ASEAN’s weak non-interference approach. Colonial maps, ICJ rulings and great-power competition keep the Preah Vihear dispute simmering—and regional peace precarious.  

### 📋 Executive Summary  
In May and December 2025, longstanding border tensions between Cambodia and Thailand reignited around the contested Preah Vihear temple, resulting in more than 100 fatalities and the displacement of over 500,000 civilians. Rooted in poorly drawn 1907 French-era maps and partial International Court of Justice (ICJ) verdicts (1962, 2013), the dispute has flared repeatedly since 2008. Short-lived ceasefires brokered by ASEAN and external actors—including a Trump-mediated peace declaration and U.S. tariff threats—have failed to hold, as each side accuses the other of drone incursions and treaty violations. ASEAN’s core principles of non-intervention and consensus decision-making have left it unable to enforce a lasting truce, eroding its credibility amid U.S.-China strategic competition. As Phnom Penh turns to the U.N. Security Council and bilateral border committees, analysts warn that without a binding security mechanism, the conflict is likely to persist, with ASEAN serving increasingly as an observer rather than an effective mediator.  

### 📖 Comprehensive Summary  
Background & Historical Roots  
The Cambodian-Thai frontier dispute around Preah Vihear dates to a 1907 French-Siamese treaty based on imprecise maps. After Cambodia’s 1953 independence, control of the temple mount was unclear until the ICJ’s 1962 ruling affirmed Cambodia’s sovereignty over the monument but left adjacent promontory lands unresolved. A 2013 ICJ judgment reiterated full Cambodian rights over the promontory while declining to demarcate the surrounding border, perpetuating ambiguity over a 4.6 km² area.

Escalation in 2025  
Tensions resurfaced in early 2025 during ceremonial provocations at Preah Vihear, then exploded in May with skirmishes at Chong Bok, an undemarcated hillock. July’s full-scale exchange—artillery, air strikes and drone incursions—claimed 32 lives and triggered U.S. President Trump’s threatened tariffs (ultimately set at 19–36%). A July peace declaration, co-signed by Cambodia’s Hun Sen and Thailand’s government under ASEAN auspices, established observer teams, prisoner releases and weapons withdrawals. Yet within weeks Thailand suspended the truce, citing unverified Cambodian drone flights, and fresh fighting in December left at least 101 dead and hundreds of thousands displaced on both sides.

ASEAN’s Constraints & External Actors  
ASEAN’s “non-interference” norm and reliance on consensus have hampered swift, binding collective action. Cambodia’s referral of the dispute to the U.N. Security Council underscores its lack of faith in ASEAN as a security guarantor. Meanwhile, U.S.-China rivalry deepens regional stakes: Washington’s tariff leverage and Beijing’s trilateral diplomatic push illustrate how external powers shape—but cannot resolve—local rivalries. ASEAN’s inability to enforce the 1976 Treaty of Amity and Cooperation or forge a binding code of conduct highlights its evolution into a forum of dialogue rather than decisive peacemaker.

Future Outlook  
Scenarios range from a protracted low-intensity stalemate—driven by entrenched nationalism and historical grievances—to rare breakthroughs via bilateral border-demarcation bodies like the Joint Boundary Commission. A lasting settlement appears improbable without ASEAN revising its non-intervention doctrine or external powers imposing a binding framework. As the Philippines assumes the 2025 ASEAN chairmanship, observers will watch whether it can pivot the bloc toward more robust security mechanisms amid South China Sea and Myanmar crises.

## Article 2: Thailand and Cambodia are bickering over their borders again. Can diplomacy prevent a return to war?

**Date:** May 2026

### 📱 Headline Summary (Tweet-length)  
After two land skirmishes in 2025 displaced half a million, Thailand scraps its 2001 maritime MOU with Cambodia. Phnom Penh invokes UNCLOS compulsory conciliation—echoing the Timor Sea precedent—to avoid war over Gulf oil and gas stakes.  

### 📋 Executive Summary  
Following brief land fights in 2025, Thailand and Cambodia reignite their border dispute—this time offshore in the Gulf of Thailand. Bangkok’s cancellation of the 2001 joint-development MOU has prompted Cambodia to ratify UNCLOS and initiate compulsory conciliation, the binding-in-process, non-binding-in-outcome mechanism last used by Timor-Leste and Australia in 2016–17. At issue are overlapping claims over fisheries and untapped Pattani Basin oil and gas reserves. Success hinges on Thailand’s willingness to engage: though legally compelled to participate, Bangkok may balk, fearing loss of maritime territory and political blowback at home. Drawing lessons from the Timor Sea case—tight deadlines, pragmatic conciliators, confidence-building separate sessions—the article argues diplomacy still offers a path to resource sharing and stable bilateral relations, even if the final recommendations remain advisory.  

### 📖 Comprehensive Summary  
Land to Sea: Shifting Frontlines  
In 2025, two brief but violent land border clashes along the 800-km Thailand-Cambodia frontier killed dozens and uprooted 500,000 people. Attention now pivots to a disputed maritime zone in the Gulf of Thailand. Both capitals trace their claims to divergent readings of a 1907 colonial-era land treaty: Cambodia’s claim bisects Thailand’s Koh Kut island, while Thailand excludes Cambodia’s offshore isles. Beneath the waves lie significant oil and gas prospects in the Pattani Basin—still unexploited due to legal uncertainty.

The 2001 MOU: “Agreement to Agree”  
The 2001 memorandum of understanding functioned as a framework to negotiate a median line in the north and joint exploitation in the south. Deadlocked talks and repeat border flare-ups led Thailand to withdraw in early 2026, citing lack of progress and bilateral trust deficits.

UNCLOS Ratification & Compulsory Conciliation  
Cambodia’s recent UNCLOS ratification unlocks Article 298’s compulsory conciliation. A first in Southeast Asia, it obliges Thailand to appoint conciliators and engage in good faith, though the final commission report is non-binding. Cambodia frames the process as a peaceful alternative to war; Thailand’s refusal to recognize ICJ jurisdiction on land disputes suggests it may resist maritime conciliation.

Lessons from the Timor Sea  
In 2016–17, Australia and Timor-Leste’s conciliation commission overcame acrimony through:  
- Tight deadlines (12–18 months)  
- Neutral, proactive conciliators who held separate, confidential meetings  
- Confidence-building measures that de-escalated tensions  
- Deferral of the most contentious issues (pipeline routing, revenue sharing)  

Timor-Leste secured 90% of Greater Sunrise revenues and a defined boundary—in non-binding terms that nonetheless set the stage for a 2018 treaty. These precedents illustrate how compulsory conciliation can reset bilateral ties and unlock resources without resorting to force.

Obstacles & Outlook  
Thailand may balk at a process imposed by a neighbor that joined UNCLOS only in 2026. Rejecting conciliation risks international censure and reinforces perceptions of Thailand’s selective adherence to rules-based order. Conversely, participation could yield access to lucrative seabed reserves and rehabilitate mutual trust. Even with a non-binding outcome, a clear roadmap can guide negotiations and reduce the risk of renewed hostilities. Diplomacy, while imperfect, remains Cambodia’s deliberate choice over conflict—even as nationalism and domestic politics threaten to derail compromise.

**Date:** August 21, 2025  
**Original URL:** https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/clyrx53zeg4o  

### 📱 Headline Summary (Tweet-length)  
Though guns are silent along the Thai-Cambodia border, an intense propaganda war rages: Cambodian rockets, disinformation guerrilla tactics, Thai political leaks and mine-laying accusations fuel nationalist fervor and deepen mistrust.  

### 📋 Executive Summary  
After a three-week truce following Cambodia’s July rocket barrage and Thai air strikes, both capitals wage an unprecedented information war. Cambodia’s Hun Sen/Manet leverages state media and social platforms to amplify allegations—downing Thai F-16s and poison gas use—while Thailand’s fragmented official statements fail to coordinate. A leaked call between PM Paetongtarn Shinawatra and Hun Sen deepens Bangkok’s political turmoil, pitting the civilian government against a nationalist military under Lt. Gen. Boonsin Padklang. Phnom Penh appeals to the UN Security Council and ICJ; Bangkok insists on bilateral talks. Mutual mine accusations under the Ottawa Convention add a humanitarian dimension. Rising nationalist narratives have displaced hundreds of thousands of migrant workers, threatening economic ties and regional stability.  

### 📖 Comprehensive Summary  
Silent Arms, Loud Words  
By mid-August 2025, the front-line guns in the forested Preah Vihear zone fell silent, but a ferocious disinformation campaign erupted online and in traditional media. Cambodia’s launch of rockets on 24 July marked the first heavy artillery use, prompting Thai air strikes and civilian casualties. Both sides swiftly pivoted to narratives: Phnom Penh’s state-backed English-language outlets and social-media “warriors” disseminated false images—a Ukrainian jet crash and Californian wildfire retardant mislabeled as Thai poison gas. Thailand’s responses, issued by disparate ministries, lacked coherence and speed, hampering Bangkok’s ability to shape international opinion.

Domestic Politics & Personal Rivalries  
The clash exposed deep rifts in Thailand’s political landscape. Prime Minister Paetongtarn Shinawatra’s plea—leaked by Hun Sen—to rein in Lt. Gen. Boonsin Padklang led to her suspension by the constitutional court, undercutting civilian authority. Meanwhile, Hun Sen, though officially retired, commanded public attention via frequent Facebook posts in uniform, framing Cambodia as the aggrieved party.

Legal Maneuvers & Internationalization  
Thailand maintains that the 25-year-old Joint Boundary Commission should resolve disputes bilaterally; Cambodia rejects this, taking the issue to the UN Security Council and ICJ. Bangkok’s rejection of ICJ jurisdiction—rooted in its 1962 defeat over Preah Vihear—now leaves it vulnerable to narratives of defying international law. Cambodia’s charges of new mine-laying under the Ottawa Convention prompt Thailand to seek sanctions on demining aid; Cambodia counters with allegations of Thai cluster munitions and temple shelling.

Humanitarian & Economic Fallout  
Intense nationalist rhetoric has driven hundreds of thousands of Cambodian migrant workers out of Thailand, imperiling remittance flows and labor markets. Thousands of civilians remain displaced on both sides. Demining experts showcased freshly buried PMN-2 mines along the border, illustrating how weapon accusations accentuate the information war’s lethal stakes.

National Identity & Stalemate  
The dispute transcends territory: Preah Vihear is a symbol of historical pride for both nations. Bangkok’s refusal to accept external adjudication and Phnom Penh’s media-savvy pluralism mean each side can neither back down nor cede narrative control. Analysts warn that without credible, coordinated communication and genuine political compromise, the war of words will perpetuate hostilities and prevent a sustainable diplomatic solution.
